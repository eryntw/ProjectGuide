Developing composite indicators for species risk assessment

4 Exposure

Definition: Exposure is defined as the rate, magnitude, or intensity of environmental change, stressors, or human-driven disturbances experienced by a species or population.

4.1 Identification of Exposure Pathways

The broad impacts of renewable energy developments on semi-arid ecosystems in Australia are outlined in the Introduction-Ecosystem Impacts.

This exposure analysis focuses on species-level responses to key stressors associated with renewable energy infrastructure, including photovoltaic (PV) solar farms, concentrated solar power (CSP) facilities, and onshore wind farms. A comprehensive overview of these impact pathways is provided in IUCN—Mitigating biodiversity impacts associated with solar and wind energy development: guidelines for project developers (Bennun2021?).

4.1.1 Impact Pathway Diagram

The impacts associated with the project footprint can be visualised using an impact pathway diagram (IPD). Grounded in conceptual modelling approaches, an IPD provides a systematic visual framework for integrating diverse sources of scientific evidence and illustrating how stressors originating from project activities propagate through the environment to affect ecological receptors (IESC2024?).

4.1.2 Exposure analysis diagram

The following narrative provides a comprehensive explanation of the IPD_main diagram, functioning as an “evidence scaffold” to organise and synthesise predicted impacts from the development source down to terminal biodiversity outcomes. The model is structured as a 10 tier hierarchy incorporating seven distinct node types to distinguish between anthropogenic drivers, sources, environmental and ecological stressors, additional steps in causal pathway (source to stressor, stressor to receptor), and biological responses.

High-Level Driver and Sources (Levels 1–2)

The primary Driver initiating this impact pathway is Renewable Energy Development. This driver focuses on two primary Tier 1 Sources: Solar farms and Wind farms. These projects are further decomposed into Tier 2 Infrastructure categories: Solar infrastructure, Wind infrastructure, and Shared infrastructure (elements required by both, such as access tracks and grid connections).

Development Activities (Levels 3–4)

  • Tier 3 Assets (Level 3): The physical entities established on-site include PV panels and Evaporation ponds (Solar), Wind towers & turbines (Wind), and Roads and Powerline (Shared). These sources can also act as stressors (threat/ impact variables).

  • Vegetation Clearance (Level 4): A critical additional step in the causal chain is the physical removal of native flora to facilitate construction. This activity acts as a major precursor to Habitat Loss and Fragmentation, altering Fire frequency and intensity and opening niches for Invasive species.

Direct Processes and Maintenance (Level 5)

  • Biological Traps: Physical assets create direct hazards such as Collision (turbines/panels), Drowning (evaporation ponds), and Electrocution (powerlines).

  • Ongoing Actions: Maintenance of PV panels and Vegetation maintenance for the infrastructure ensure long-term operational viability but reinforce persistent stressors.

Proximate Stressors and Processes (Levels 6–8)

  • Infrastructure leads to change in Hydrology and Microclimate, increase in Pollutants and Fragmentation. These changes further alter Soil dynamics and transform Woodland & Shrubland into grassland or dessert.

  • The introduction of Invasive species (facilitated by roads and clearing) triggers a secondary cascade of biological stressors, including increase in Fire frequency & intensity (i.e., Buffel grass), Trampling (i.e., goats), Competition (i.e., goats and rabbits), Predation (i.e., cats), and Grazing (i.e., goats and rabbits).

  • The cumulative effect of physical stressors (soil, fragmentation) and biological pressures (fire, trampling) results in Habitat degradation, representing a decline in environmental quality.

  1. Receptors and Assessment Endpoints (Levels 10–11)
  • Responses (Level 10): The convergence of impact pathways leads to three primary negative outcomes: Direct Mortality (from infrastructure hazards), Habitat Loss (from clearing and grazing), and Population decline (resulting from predation, degradation and competition).

4.2 The Stressors

To ensure the index remains both ecologically meaningful and analytically tractable, only a subset of exposure pathways was included, based on their ecological significance, data availability, and relevance to species-level risk.

Global evidence identifies habitat loss, invasive species, and exploitation as the primary drivers of recent extinctions across 615 species (Saban and Wiens 2025). While invasive species contribute the largest proportion of extinctions globally (38.0%), habitat loss is the dominant driver in mainland systems (51.1%), with invasive species (16.5%) and exploitation (15.5%) playing secondary roles.

Within the scope of this assessment, exploitation is not considered a relevant pressure, as none of the focal species are subject to harvesting. Pollution, although recognised as an additional driver of biodiversity loss, contributes relatively little to extinction risk (5.1% globally; 11.4% for mainland extinctions) and is therefore not prioritised here.

Accordingly, the index focuses on the two most influential and context-relevant pressures: habitat loss and invasive species. These pressures form the basis of the thematic subgroups included in the exposure analysis.

4.2.1 Invasive Species

Invasive species encompass both plants and animals, but their mechanisms of impact differ. Invasive plants primarily alter ecosystem structure and resource availability, whereas invasive animals exert direct pressures through predation and competition. In arid systems, invasive plants are likely to pose substantial risks due to their capacity to transform vegetation structure and ecosystem processes at broad spatial scales.

Invasive plants: trait-based vulnerability framework

In arid ecosystems, the establishment success of alien plant species is mediated by resource availability and functional similarity to native species. Specifically, alien species that are functionally dissimilar to native communities tend to establish more successfully in resource-rich environments, whereas establishment success declines for species that are functionally similar in resource-limited systems (El-Barougy et al. 2021).

Building on this framework, vulnerability to plant invasion can be quantified using functional trait approaches. A set of ecologically relevant traits can be used to calculate the Alien–Native Mean Functional Distance (ANMFD), which provides a proxy for the susceptibility of native communities to invasion.

Applying this approach to Australian arid-zone ecosystems would enable the prediction of native species vulnerability within a trait-based framework. While robust, this method requires extensive trait data and is therefore beyond the immediate scope of this assessment. Nevertheless, it represents a promising direction for future work and could form the basis of a standalone analysis (e.g., “Predicting native species vulnerability to invasive plants in the Australian arid zone”).

Invasive predators and herbivores affecting native animals

Although invasive plants can indirectly influence fauna—by filtering bird assemblages, facilitating further invasion, and reducing phylogenetic and functional diversity (Grzędzicka et al. 2024; Izquierdo-Palma et al. 2025)—these interactions are highly context dependent and typically require detailed field-based studies. As a result, the present analysis focuses on invasive animal species that exert more direct and generalisable impacts on native fauna.

Specifically, four widespread invasive mammals are considered: the predators cats (Felis catus) and foxes (Vulpes vulpes), and the herbivore competitors rabbits (Oryctolagus cuniculus) and camels (Camelus dromedarius). These species are well established in arid Australia and have documented, system-wide impacts on native vertebrates.

Invasive predators

Both foxes and cats exhibit size-selective predation on mammals, with peak predation occurring at approximately 280 g for foxes and 130 g for cats (Stobo-Wilson et al. 2021). In birds, susceptibility is also mediated by body size: larger species (≥3.4 kg) are more vulnerable to fox predation, whereas smaller species (<3.4 kg) are more susceptible to cats (Woinarski et al. 2022). Additional traits further modify risk, with cat predation highest among bird species that are island-restricted, of intermediate body mass (ca. 60–300 g), and that nest or forage on the ground (Woinarski et al. 2017).

In arid ecosystems, cats are likely to represent the dominant invasive predator threat to birds. This is due to their higher predicted densities and greater rates of avian predation relative to foxes (Stobo-Wilson et al. 2022). In contrast, fox diets are more heavily dominated by mammals (approximately 70%) (Stobo-Wilson et al. 2021), and their abundance tends to be higher in more mesic or coastal environments (Stobo-Wilson et al. 2022).

Invasive Herbivores

A range of native and introduced herbivores occur within the pilot areas, including kangaroos, rabbits, goats, horses (northern South Australia), donkeys (northern South Australia), and camels (northern South Australia). Empirical evidence indicates that introduced herbivores—particularly livestock, rabbits, and goats—have substantial negative impacts on native plant communities, whereas kangaroo grazing is comparatively benign (Travers et al. 2018). Increasing kangaroo grazing pressure does not significantly affect plant occurrence across species origin, life span, or growth form. In contrast, livestock and rabbit grazing are associated with marked declines in native vegetation, occurring at densities of approximately 1000 kg dung/ha and 100 kg dung/ha, respectively (Travers et al. 2018).

Rabbits strongly influence vegetation dynamics by suppressing recruitment and maintaining ecosystems in early successional states. Even at low densities (1–2 individuals per hectare), they can eliminate seedlings of palatable native trees and shrubs, thereby intensifying competition with invasive plants (Finlayson et al. 2022). This impact is taxonomically selective, disproportionately affecting genera such as Allocasuarina, Acacia, Bursaria, and Senna, while less palatable species (e.g., eucalypts) may persist (Cooke2007?). Although seedling loss is often difficult to detect, chronic recruitment failure leads to long-term declines in plant diversity as mature individuals are not replaced (Cooke2007?). Functionally, rabbit herbivory resembles vegetation clearance, but operates through sustained suppression of regeneration rather than removal of established plants, typically affecting vegetation up to ~50 cm in height.

Goats represent an emerging and potentially dominant threat within the pilot areas. Recent surveys report very high abundances and extensive overgrazing, including mustering rates of ~10,000 individuals per day and an estimated regional increase of ~600,000 goats in the Braemar Province (SA Bioregional Planning Pilot Project Interim Report, Deliverable 11). Goats selectively remove palatable vegetation, inhibit recruitment, facilitate invasive plant spread, and cause substantial trampling damage, compounding impacts from rabbits. Field observations indicate severe degradation of habitats supporting multiple priority species, including Swainsona fuscoviridis, Swainsona murrayana, Brachyscome muelleri, endemic orchids, Yellow-footed Rock-wallaby, and Plains Rat.

Unlike rabbits, goats can exploit rugged terrain and browse at greater heights (~1–2 m), resulting in impacts across multiple vegetation strata. Under high grazing pressure, vegetation across all growth forms may be removed, producing ecosystem effects functionally equivalent to vegetation clearance.

4.2.2 Habitat fragmentation

species responses to habitat fragmentation is primarily driven by species’ dispersal capacity. Dispersal limitation provides the most consistent and general explanation for species responses to fragmented landscapes. In birds, morphological proxies of dispersal ability, such as the inverse hand-wing index (HWI), outperform other predictors (e.g., body mass, latitude, and historical disturbance) in explaining vulnerability (Weeks et al. 2023). Species with low dispersal capacity are therefore less able to move between habitat patches and are more susceptible to local extinction.

Habitat specialisation further amplifies fragmentation effects. Specialist species exhibit stronger population structuring and reduced persistence compared with generalists, even at relatively small spatial scales (Khimoun et al. 2016). At broader taxonomic scales, fragmentation sensitivity is also influenced by habitat type and landscape context, with higher vulnerability observed in forest and shrubland species relative to grassland taxa, as well as in larger-bodied species, carnivores, and reptiles (Keinath et al. 2017). In contrast, reproductive life-history traits (e.g., clutch or brood size) show little consistent relationship with fragmentation responses (Vargas et al. 2012).

Based on the evidence, three key variables were selected to represent sensitivity to habitat degradation:

  1. Dispersal capacity (Hand-Wing Index; HWI) (Weeks et al. 2023) HWI is a morphological proxy for flight efficiency and dispersal ability. Higher HWI values are associated with more dispersive, migratory, or aerial species, whereas lower values indicate limited dispersal capacity and increased sensitivity to fragmentation.

  2. Habitat specialisation and habitat type vulnerability (Khimoun et al. 2016; Keinath et al. 2017) Species with narrower habitat breadth are more sensitive to fragmentation. This is further modulated by habitat type, with vulnerability weighted such that forest > shrubland > grassland systems, reflecting differences in fragmentation sensitivity.

  3. Extent of occurrence (EOO) (Keinath et al. 2017) Species with smaller geographic ranges are more vulnerable to habitat loss and fragmentation due to limited spatial redundancy. EOO is incorporated using the same scoring framework applied in the sensitivity component.

4.5.1 Habitat degradation

Habitat degradation encompasses a range of indirect effects that alter environmental conditions, in addition to altered fire regime and habitat fragmentation:

  • Hydrological change: Alterations to water availability, with largely unknown species-level responses

  • Microclimate change: Localised changes in temperature, shading, and humidity, which are difficult to quantify directly

  • Pollution: Potential impacts from dust, chemicals, and other contaminants

  • Ecosystem services: Broad system-level changes that are complex and difficult to attribute at the species level

Nevertheless, these processes are challenging to quantify explicitly and are partially captured through the sensitivity component of the framework.
